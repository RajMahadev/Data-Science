<p><strong>Data science</strong>&nbsp;is an&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Inter-disciplinary" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inter-disciplinary" data-smd-id="s111">inter-disciplinary</a>&nbsp;field that uses scientific methods, processes, algorithms and systems to extract&nbsp;<a title="Knowledge" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge" data-smd-id="s112">knowledge</a>&nbsp;and insights from structured and&nbsp;<a title="Unstructured data" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unstructured_data" data-smd-id="s113">unstructured data</a>.&nbsp;Data science is related to&nbsp;<a title="Data mining" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_mining" data-smd-id="s114">data mining</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Big data" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_data" data-smd-id="s115">big data</a>.</p>
<p>Data science is a "concept to unify&nbsp;<a title="Statistics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics" data-smd-id="s116">statistics</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Data analysis" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_analysis" data-smd-id="s117">data analysis</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Machine learning" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine_learning" data-smd-id="s118">machine learning</a>&nbsp;and their related methods" in order to "understand and analyze actual phenomena" with data.&nbsp;It employs techniques and theories drawn from many fields within the context of&nbsp;<a title="Mathematics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics" data-smd-id="s119">mathematics</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Statistics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics" data-smd-id="s120">statistics</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Computer science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science" data-smd-id="s121">computer science</a>, and&nbsp;<a title="Information science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_science" data-smd-id="s122">information science</a>.&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Turing award" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turing_award" data-smd-id="s123">Turing award</a>&nbsp;winner&nbsp;<a title="Jim Gray (computer scientist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jim_Gray_(computer_scientist)" data-smd-id="s124">Jim Gray</a>&nbsp;imagined data science as a "fourth paradigm" of science (<a title="Empirical research" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical_research" data-smd-id="s125">empirical</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Basic research" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basic_research" data-smd-id="s126">theoretical</a>,&nbsp;<a title="Computational science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_science" data-smd-id="s127">computational</a>&nbsp;and now data-driven) and asserted that "everything about science is changing because of the impact of information technology" and the&nbsp;<a title="Information explosion" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_explosion" data-smd-id="s128">data deluge</a>. In 2015, the&nbsp;<a title="American Statistical Association" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Statistical_Association" data-smd-id="s129">American Statistical Association</a>&nbsp;identified&nbsp;<a title="Database" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Database" data-smd-id="s130">database management</a>, statistics and&nbsp;<a title="Machine learning" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine_learning" data-smd-id="s131">machine learning</a>, and&nbsp;<a title="Distributed computing" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributed_computing" data-smd-id="s132">distributed and parallel systems</a>&nbsp;as the three emerging foundational professional communities.</p>

<h2><span id="History" class="mw-headline">History</span></h2>
<p>The term "data science" has appeared in various contexts over the past thirty years but did not become an established term until recently. In an early usage, it was used as a substitute for&nbsp;<a title="Computer science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science" data-smd-id="s134">computer science</a>&nbsp;by&nbsp;<a title="Peter Naur" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Naur" data-smd-id="s135">Peter Naur</a>&nbsp;in 1960. Naur later introduced the term "datalogy".&nbsp;In 1974, Naur published&nbsp;<em>Concise Survey of Computer Methods</em>, which freely used the term data science in its survey of the contemporary data processing methods that are used in a wide range of applications.</p>
<p>The modern definition of "data science" was first sketched during the second Japanese-French statistics symposium organized at the&nbsp;<a title="Montpellier 2 University" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Montpellier_2_University" data-smd-id="s136">University of Montpellier II</a>&nbsp;(France) in 1992. The attendees acknowledged the emergence of a new discipline with a specific focus on data from various origins, dimensions, types and structures. They shaped the contour of this new science based on established concepts and principles of statistics and data analysis with the extensive use of the increasing power of computer tools.</p>
<p>In 1996, members of the International Federation of Classification Societies (IFCS) met in Kobe for their biennial conference. Here, for the first time, the term data science is included in the title of the conference ("Data Science, classification, and related methods"),&nbsp;after the term was introduced in a roundtable discussion by Chikio Hayashi.</p>
<p>In November 1997,&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="C.F. Jeff Wu" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C.F._Jeff_Wu" data-smd-id="s137">C.F. Jeff Wu</a>&nbsp;gave the inaugural lecture entitled "Statistics = Data Science?"&nbsp;for his appointment to the H. C. Carver Professorship at the&nbsp;<a title="University of Michigan" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Michigan" data-smd-id="s138">University of Michigan</a>.&nbsp;In this lecture, he characterized statistical work as a trilogy of data collection, data modeling and analysis, and decision making. In his conclusion, he initiated the modern, non-computer science, usage of the term "data science" and advocated that statistics be renamed data science and statisticians data scientists.&nbsp;Later, he presented his lecture entitled "Statistics = Data Science?" as the first of his 1998 P.C. Mahalanobis Memorial Lectures.&nbsp;These lectures honor&nbsp;<a title="Prasanta Chandra Mahalanobis" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prasanta_Chandra_Mahalanobis" data-smd-id="s139">Prasanta Chandra Mahalanobis</a>, an Indian scientist and statistician and founder of the&nbsp;<a title="Indian Statistical Institute" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Statistical_Institute" data-smd-id="s140">Indian Statistical Institute</a>.</p>
<p>In 2001, William S. Cleveland introduced data science as an independent discipline, extending the field of statistics to incorporate "advances in computing with data" in his article "Data Science: An Action Plan for Expanding the Technical Areas of the Field of Statistics," which was published in Volume 69, No. 1, of the April 2001 edition of the International Statistical Review / Revue Internationale de Statistique.&nbsp;In his report, Cleveland establishes six technical areas which he believed to encompass the field of data science: multidisciplinary investigations, models and methods for data, computing with data, pedagogy, tool evaluation, and theory.</p>
<p>In April 2002, the International Council for Science (ICSU): Committee on Data for Science and Technology (CODATA)&nbsp;started the&nbsp;<em>Data Science Journal</em>,&nbsp;a publication focused on issues such as the description of data systems, their publication on the internet, applications and legal issues.&nbsp;Shortly thereafter, in January 2003, Columbia University began publishing&nbsp;<em>The Journal of Data Science</em>,&nbsp;which provided a platform for all data workers to present their views and exchange ideas. The journal was largely devoted to the application of statistical methods and quantitative research. In 2005, The National Science Board published "Long-lived Digital Data Collections: Enabling Research and Education in the 21st Century" defining data scientists as "the information and computer scientists, database and software and programmers, disciplinary experts, curators and expert annotators, librarians, archivists, and others, who are crucial to the successful management of a digital data collection" whose primary activity is to "conduct creative inquiry and analysis."</p>
<p>Around 2007,&nbsp;Turing award winner&nbsp;<a title="Jim Gray (computer scientist)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jim_Gray_(computer_scientist)" data-smd-id="s142">Jim Gray</a>&nbsp;envisioned "data-driven science" as a "fourth paradigm" of science that uses the computational analysis of large data as primary scientific method&nbsp;and "to have a world in which all of the science literature is online, all of the science data is online, and they interoperate with each other."</p>
<p>In the 2012&nbsp;<a title="Harvard Business Review" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvard_Business_Review" data-smd-id="s143">Harvard Business Review</a>&nbsp;article "Data Scientist: The Sexiest Job of the 21st Century",&nbsp;<a title="DJ Patil" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DJ_Patil" data-smd-id="s144">DJ Patil</a>&nbsp;claims to have coined this term in 2008 with&nbsp;<a title="Jeff Hammerbacher" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jeff_Hammerbacher" data-smd-id="s145">Jeff Hammerbacher</a>&nbsp;to define their jobs at LinkedIn and Facebook, respectively. He asserts that a data scientist is "a new breed", and that a "shortage of data scientists is becoming a serious constraint in some sectors", but describes a much more business-oriented role.</p>
<p>In 2013, the IEEE Task Force on Data Science and Advanced Analytics&nbsp;was launched. In 2013, the first "European Conference on Data Analysis (ECDA)" was organised in Luxembourg, establishing the&nbsp;<a class="external text" href="http://euads.org/" rel="nofollow" data-smd-id="s146">European Association for Data Science (EuADS)</a>. The first international conference: IEEE International Conference on Data Science and Advanced Analytics was launched in 2014.&nbsp;In 2014,&nbsp;<a title="General Assembly (school)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Assembly_(school)" data-smd-id="s147">General Assembly</a>&nbsp;launched student-paid bootcamp and&nbsp;<a title="The Data Incubator" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Data_Incubator" data-smd-id="s148">The Data Incubator</a>&nbsp;launched a competitive free data science fellowship.&nbsp;In 2014, the&nbsp;<a title="American Statistical Association" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Statistical_Association" data-smd-id="s149">American Statistical Association</a>&nbsp;section on Statistical Learning and Data Mining renamed its journal to "Statistical Analysis and Data Mining: The ASA Data Science Journal" and in 2016 changed its section name to "Statistical Learning and Data Science".&nbsp;In 2015, the International Journal on Data Science and Analytics was launched by Springer to publish original work on data science and big data analytics. In September 2015 the&nbsp;<a class="external text" href="http://www.gfkl.org/welcome/" rel="nofollow" data-smd-id="s150">Gesellschaft f&uuml;r Klassifikation (GfKl)</a>&nbsp;added to the name of the Society "Data Science Society" at the third ECDA conference at the&nbsp;<a title="University of Essex" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Essex" data-smd-id="s151">University of Essex</a>, Colchester, UK.</p>
<h2><span id="Relationship_to_statistics" class="mw-headline">Relationship to statistics</span></h2>
<p>In the question-and-answer section of his keynote address at the Joint Statistical Meetings of&nbsp;<a title="American Statistical Association" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Statistical_Association" data-smd-id="s153">American Statistical Association</a>, noted applied statistician&nbsp;<a title="Nate Silver" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nate_Silver" data-smd-id="s154">Nate Silver</a>&nbsp;said, "I think data-scientist is a sexed up term for a statistician....Statistics is a branch of science. Data scientist is slightly redundant in some way and people shouldn&rsquo;t berate the term statistician."</p>
<p>On the other hand, responses to criticism are as numerous. In a 2014&nbsp;<a title="The Wall Street Journal" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" data-smd-id="s155">Wall Street Journal</a>&nbsp;article, Irving Wladawsky-Berger compares the data science enthusiasm with the dawn of&nbsp;<a title="Computer science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science" data-smd-id="s156">computer science</a>. He argues data science, like any other&nbsp;<a title="Interdisciplinarity" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interdisciplinarity" data-smd-id="s157">interdisciplinary</a>&nbsp;field, employs&nbsp;<a title="Methodology" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methodology" data-smd-id="s158">methodologies</a>&nbsp;and practices from across the&nbsp;<a title="Academy" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academy" data-smd-id="s159">academia</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Industry" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industry" data-smd-id="s160">industry</a>, but then it will morph them into a new&nbsp;<a title="Discipline (academia)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discipline_(academia)" data-smd-id="s161">discipline</a>. He brings to attention the sharp criticisms of computer science, now a well respected academic discipline, had to once face.&nbsp;Likewise,&nbsp;<a title="New York University" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_University" data-smd-id="s162">NYU</a>&nbsp;<a title="NYU Stern Center for Business and Human Rights" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NYU_Stern_Center_for_Business_and_Human_Rights" data-smd-id="s163">Stern's</a>&nbsp;Vasant Dhar, as do many other academic proponents of data science,&nbsp;argues more specifically in December 2013 that data science is different from the existing practice of data analysis across all&nbsp;<a title="Discipline (academia)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discipline_(academia)" data-smd-id="s164">disciplines</a>, which focuses only on explaining&nbsp;<a title="Data set" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_set" data-smd-id="s165">data sets</a>. Data science seeks actionable and consistent&nbsp;<a title="Pattern recognition" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pattern_recognition" data-smd-id="s166">pattern</a>&nbsp;for&nbsp;<a title="Predictive modelling" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Predictive_modelling" data-smd-id="s167">predictive uses</a>. This practical engineering goal takes data science beyond traditional&nbsp;<a title="Analytics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytics" data-smd-id="s168">analytics</a>. Now the data in those disciplines and&nbsp;<a title="Applied science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Applied_science" data-smd-id="s169">applied fields</a>&nbsp;that lacked solid&nbsp;<a title="Theory" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory" data-smd-id="s170">theories</a>, like&nbsp;<a class="mw-redirect" title="Health science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_science" data-smd-id="s171">health science</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a title="Social science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_science" data-smd-id="s172">social science</a>, could be sought and utilized to generate powerful predictive models.</p>
<p>In an effort similar to Dhar's, Stanford professor&nbsp;<a title="David Donoho" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Donoho" data-smd-id="s173">David Donoho</a>, in September 2015, takes the proposition further by rejecting three simplistic and misleading definitions of data science in lieu of criticisms.&nbsp;First, for Donoho, data science does not equate to&nbsp;<a title="Big data" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_data" data-smd-id="s174">big data</a>, in that the size of the data set is not a criterion to distinguish data science and statistics.&nbsp;Second, data science is not defined by the&nbsp;<a title="Computing" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computing" data-smd-id="s175">computing</a>&nbsp;skills of sorting big data sets, in that these skills are already generally used for analyses across all disciplines.&nbsp;Third, data science is a heavily applied field where&nbsp;<a title="Graduate school" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graduate_school" data-smd-id="s176">academic programs</a>&nbsp;right now do not sufficiently prepare data scientists for the jobs, in that many&nbsp;<a title="Graduate school" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graduate_school" data-smd-id="s177">graduate programs</a>&nbsp;misleadingly advertise their&nbsp;<a title="Analytics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytics" data-smd-id="s178">analytics</a>&nbsp;and statistics training as the essence of a data science program.&nbsp;As a&nbsp;<a title="Statistician" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistician" data-smd-id="s179">statistician</a>,&nbsp;<a title="David Donoho" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Donoho" data-smd-id="s180">Donoho</a>, following many in his field, champions the broadening of learning scope in the form of data science,&nbsp;like John Chambers who urges statisticians to adopt an inclusive concept of learning from data.&nbsp;Together, these&nbsp;<a title="Statistician" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistician" data-smd-id="s181">statisticians</a>&nbsp;envision an increasingly inclusive applied field that grows out of traditional&nbsp;<a title="Statistics" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics" data-smd-id="s182">statistics</a>&nbsp;and beyond.</p>
<p>For the future of data science, Donoho projects an ever-growing environment for&nbsp;<a title="Open science" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_science" data-smd-id="s183">open science</a>&nbsp;where data sets used for&nbsp;<a title="Academic publishing" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_publishing" data-smd-id="s184">academic publications</a>&nbsp;are accessible to all researchers.<span style="font-size: xx-small;">&nbsp;</span><a title="National Institutes of Health" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" data-smd-id="s185">US National Institute of Health</a>&nbsp;has already announced plans to enhance reproducibility and transparency of research data.&nbsp;Other big&nbsp;<a title="Academic journal" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_journal" data-smd-id="s186">journals</a>&nbsp;are likewise following suit.&nbsp;This way, the future of data science not only exceeds the boundary of&nbsp;<a title="Statistical theory" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_theory" data-smd-id="s187">statistical theories</a>&nbsp;in scale and methodology, but data science will revolutionize current academia and&nbsp;<a title="Paradigm" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paradigm" data-smd-id="s188">research paradigms</a>.&nbsp;As Donoho concludes, "the scope and impact of data science will continue to expand enormously in coming decades as scientific data and data about science itself become ubiquitously available."</p>
